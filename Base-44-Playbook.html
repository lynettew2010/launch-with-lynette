Section 1: Credit Conservation
The First Thing to Understand

Base44 credits burn fast when you are just prompting without a plan. I learned this the expensive way. Before you build a single feature, understand how credits work so you budget like cash.

Base44 has two modes:

Chat Mode

Chat mode is full build mode. It burns more credits because it is actually writing and modifying code. Use this only when you are ready to build something.

Ask a Question

Ask a Question mode is consultation mode. It uses significantly fewer credits.

Use this for planning, research, outlining, troubleshooting, and any time you need to think through a decision without building yet.

A 30-minute planning session in Ask a Question mode can save 200 or more credits compared to prompting in Chat without a plan.

💡 The rule: think in Ask a Question, build in Chat.
☝🏾 One more thing

Screenshot every working version of your app before making major changes. Base44 updates can sometimes affect existing features. A screenshot is your record of what worked.

A written prompt history is even better.

Save your successful prompts in a separate document as you go.

You will use them again on future builds.

Section 2: The Pre-Build Phase
Do Not Skip This

Step 1: Ideation

Start with your top three ideas. Get pen to paper. Do not overthink.

The best software solves a problem you have, or a problem someone you know has.

I built Launch Signal Pro because I was solving my own follow-up problem.

In every business I had run, I would get leads, make contact once, and then forget. By the time I followed up, the prospect had moved on.

I had expensive CRMs.

Nothing worked.

ADHD made it worse.

So I built a system that follows up automatically. You may not take that approach.

But if you know business owners, friends, family, alumni, start there.

  • Ask them what they do day to day
  • What they like about their current tools
  • What they wish was better
  • Look for operational gaps
  • Software gaps
  • Process gaps
  • communication gaps
💡 The $1,000,000 Test:

If someone offered you one million dollars to explain your software in 60 seconds:

What it does?

Who it is for?

Could you do it clearly?

If not, keep refining.

You need to be able to answer three questions without hesitating.

  1. What does it do?
  2. Who is it for?
  3. What problem does it solve?

When you can answer all three in under a minute, you are ready to move forward.

Section 3: Design and Feature Planning Before You Open Base44

Most people open Base44 and start prompting immediately.

🙋🏾‍♀️ This was me!

Do not do this.

Spend time designing before you build.

It saves you from building the wrong thing and then having to tear it down.

Visual Design:

If you are a visual person, sketch your layout on paper first.

Every screen, every button, every navigation element.

If you want something more polished, use Figma or a similar tool.

The goal is to have a picture of what you are building before Base44 writes a single line of code.

Choose your brand colors before you start.

I use a dark background with pink and purple accents on LSP.

Picking colors before you build means your app looks intentional from the first version.

Changing colors after the fact across a whole app is painful.

Feature Planning:

Write out every feature you want the app to have.

Then cut the list in half.

Start with MVP features only. You can always add more later.

A working app with five features ships.

An app trying to do thirty things at once never does.

🙋🏾‍♀️ Also me. Notice a pattern?

Features to Consider:

  • Save options
  • Reports
  • Formatting
  • File types
  • Search
  • Filters
  • Sorting
  • User roles
  • Notifications
  • Dashboards
  • Analytics
  • Export and Import

Integrations to Consider:

  • Email tools like Gmail or Google Workspace
  • Calendar and booking tools like Calendly
  • Automation tools like Zapier
  • Payment processing like Stripe
  • CRM and forms

Think about what your users already use. The best integrations are the ones that slot into existing workflows.

LSP integrates with Instantly for cold email sequences.

That integration is the core of the outreach system.

Thinking about it before I built saved me from designing a feature that would have conflicted with it later.

Section 4: Adding AI Agents to Your Build

Think of an agent as your AI employee inside the software.

It has skills you define.

It operates in those skills consistently.

This is one of the most powerful things you can build into a Base44 app, and one of the most underused by first-time builders.

Skills to consider for your agents:

  • Marketing Strategy,
  • Sales
  • Copywriting
  • Content Creation
  • Social Media Management
  • SEO Optimization
  • Data Analysis
  • Customer Support
  • Lead Qualification
  • Appointment Setting

The right skills depend entirely on what your software does.

LSP has three agents. The audit analyzer reviews websites and identifies gaps. The outreach generator turns audit findings into personalized cold email copy. The follow-up generator creates nurture messages for leads at different stages of the conversation. Three specialists. Each one trained deeply in one domain. Much better output than one generalist agent trying to do all three.

The most important decision when building agents:

Define the domain before you build the agent.

  • What is this agent an expert in?
  • What does it do and nothing else?

If you cannot answer that in one sentence, the agent is too broad.

☝🏾 One more thing about agents that I wish I had known earlier

The quality of your agent's output is directly tied to the quality of your instructions.

Generic instructions produce generic output.

Specific rules, banned phrases, before and after examples, a clear output format, all of this makes the agent produce output that actually sounds like what you intended.

The section on prompting in this playbook covers this in more detail.

Section 5: Prompting Strategy for Base44 Chat

Never open Chat mode without a written outline.

Every time I skipped this step, I wasted credits rebuilding something that did not connect properly to what already existed.

Outline First Approach

Here is the outline structure I use before every major build session:

APP OVERVIEW Build a [type] app that helps [ICP] solve [problem] by [solution]. CORE FEATURES LIST Feature 1: [description] Feature 2: [description] Feature 3: [description] SCREEN LAYOUT Screen 1: Login Screen 2: Dashboard with [specific elements] Screen 3: [name] with [specific elements] INTEGRATIONS Connect to [API or service] for [function] The connection should [specific behavior] AGENT SKILLS Add an agent that can [skill 1] and [skill 2]. It should [specific behavior] and never [specific limitation] The more specific your outline, the better Base44's output. Vague prompts produce vague software.

Prompt Etiquette

One feature per prompt.

Do not dump twenty requests into one message.

Base44 will attempt all of them, connect them poorly, and you will spend twice the credits fixing the mess.

Test after every build step before adding complexity.

If the feature you just built does not work correctly, do not add the next one on top of it.

Debug first, then expand. Use Ask a Question to troubleshoot.

When something breaks, switch to Ask a Question mode, describe the issue, and ask Base44 to diagnose it.

You get the same answer for fewer credits. Give Base44 full context before every build request. Tell it what you already have built, what you want to add, and what you do not want it to change.

The three-part structure that works:

  • Context: “I already have [X built]. It works by doing [Y].
  • Goal: "I want to add [Z] that connects to [X] by doing [W]."
  • Constraint: "Do not change [A], [B], or [C]. Those are working correctly."

Save successful prompts in a document as you go. You will reuse them.

On your next build you will start from a library instead of starting from scratch.

Section 6: Three Lessons I Learned Building Launch Signal Pro

Three Lessons

These are the same three lessons I share in my YouTube series.

I repeat them here because they are the ones that actually mattered.

Lesson 1: Have an outline

My ADHD brain wanted to jump straight in. I love building. I love debugging. I could do it for hours without stopping.

But jumping in without a plan meant I built features that did not connect, tabs that did not belong, and logic that made sense to me but nobody else.

If you want a SaaS and not just a prototype, you also have to think about scale from the start.

Can a real developer come in after you and understand what you built? If your code is a mess of half-finished features and inconsistent naming, that is a problem you will pay for later.

Have the outline before you touch Chat mode.

It is the highest-leverage thing you can do before you build.

Lesson 2: Build agents with skills

I wish I had known this at the beginning.

I built a general-purpose agent first and the output was mediocre across everything it tried to do. Specialist agents produce dramatically better output.

One agent for one domain.

Trained deeply with specific rules, banned phrases, and before and after examples.

The more specific the training, the better the output.

If you are building anything with AI inside it, this is the thing that separates a tool people tolerate from a tool people recommend.

Lesson 3: Do not overcomplicate

But know what you are signing up for. I am the queen of trials. I will try any tool if I can get a free trial. I built on trial plans to prove to myself I could do it and to understand what was actually possible before committing money.

If you do not have a specific use case yet, do your market research first.

Make sure the problem is real before you build the solution.
